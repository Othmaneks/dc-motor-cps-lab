Understanding the bench before building it

A from-scratch explanation of every part and idea behind identifying a DC motor — written for an industrial engineer, not an electronics major.

Othmane Ksir · ENSGSI 1AI · 2025–2026 · status: hardware purchased, build pending

The goal of this project is to take a real DC motor and work out its mathematical model from measured data — its inertia, its friction, its electrical behaviour. Before any of that, I need to genuinely understand what I'm holding. This page is that understanding, built up one idea at a time.

Concept 1

Electrical basics — think of water

Everything else rests on three words. The easiest way in is to picture water flowing through a pipe.

The water analogy mental model

Voltage (volts, V)The pressure pushing the electricity. My supply pushes at 12 V.
Current (amps, A)The amount flowing. The motor "drinks" up to 5.5 A when working hard.
Ground (GND)The reference level, like sea-level for measuring height. Every voltage is measured relative to it. Remember this — it returns.

A motor converts pressure-and-flow into spinning. More voltage → spins faster. More load fighting it → it draws more current. That's the whole intuition.

Concept 2 & 3

The motor, and the muscle it needs

Apply voltage to the motor's two thick wires and it spins. Swap which wire is + and which is −, and it spins the other way. Direction is just polarity. Simple.

The catch: the Arduino brain is a weakling. It pushes only milliamps (thousandths of an amp); the motor wants whole amps — thousands of times more. Wire the motor straight to the Arduino and you get nothing, or you destroy the Arduino. So a muscle sits in between: the driver.

The H-bridge — four switches in an "H"

Picture four switches arranged in an H, with the motor as the middle bar. Closing different pairs sends current through the motor one way or the other:

Switches closedResult
top-left + bottom-rightcurrent flows one way → forward
top-right + bottom-leftcurrent flows the other way → backward

I never touch these switches. The Arduino flips them through two pins called INA and INB — think of them as a little steering wheel: one combination forward, the other reverse. That's direction control.

PWM — controlling speed with a fast on/off flick

How do you run the motor at half speed? Not by halving the voltage (hard to do cleanly) — instead by flicking the power on and off very fast, hundreds of times a second. This is PWM (Pulse-Width Modulation):

On-timeMotor feels
on 100%full speed
on 50%, off 50%≈ half speed
on 20%slow

The motor can't react to each flick — it feels the average. Like tapping a gas pedal lightly and often to cruise slowly. The on-fraction is the duty cycle, set on the Arduino as a number 0–255 (0 = off, 255 = full, 128 ≈ half). It comes out of a special pin marked ~.

The full control picture remember

INA / INB set direction · PWM sets speed. Three wires from Arduino to driver and I command the motor completely.

Concept 4 · the heart of the project

The encoder — the eyes

The motor spins, but it doesn't tell me how fast or how far. The encoder does. It's a sensor on the back of the shaft that turns spinning into countable pulses.

Picture a disc on the shaft with tiny marks, and a sensor that goes "tick!" each time a mark passes. Count ticks → how far it turned. Count ticks per second → how fast. That's the core idea.

Two channels = direction and distance

There are actually two sensors, set slightly apart — channel A and channel B. Both tick as the shaft turns, but one always ticks just before the other:

Which ticks firstMeans
A before Bturning forward
B before Aturning backward

Together they give both how far and which way. (The fancy word is "quadrature" — but the idea is just: two offset sensors.)

The six wires

Motor + — thick, takes 12 V
Motor − — thick, takes 12 V
Encoder Vcc — thin, 5 V from Arduino
Encoder GND — thin
Channel A — thin, signal
Channel B — thin, signal

Handling rule that prevents the worst mistake danger

Thick wires = 12 V brawn. Thin wires = delicate 5 V signals. Never put 12 V on the thin encoder wires — it destroys the encoder instantly. The encoder's power comes from the Arduino's 5 V, never from the supply.

Concept 5 · the thing beginners forget

Common ground — everyone agrees on sea level

Recall: ground is the reference "sea level." I have two power sources — the 12 V supply (motor) and the USB/Arduino (5 V brain). Each has its own ground.

Problem: when the Arduino sends a signal to the driver, that signal is a voltage — but measured against what? Against ground. If the two grounds sit at different "sea levels," the driver can't read the Arduino correctly. It's two people describing a height while disagreeing on where the floor is.

The fix burn this in

Connect all grounds together with a wire. Now everyone agrees on sea level and signals make sense. Skip this single wire and nothing works — and you won't know why.
